# Korean Hanji Papermaking Traditions Registry

This repository preserves, in an immutable and decentralized format, the papermaking traditions of **hanji** — Korea’s mulberry-based paper renowned for its durability, translucency, and longevity.

Hanji is produced from the inner bark of the mulberry tree (*dak*) and hollyhock mucilage (*dakpul*), using techniques such as **webal** (single-sieve sheet formation) and **ssangbal** (double-sieve method). These methods create a paper that can last for centuries without yellowing or tearing.

## Why Hanji Matters

Hanji represents:

- a **material science heritage**, using mulberry fibers with exceptional tensile strength  
- a **regional craft system**, with Jeonju, Andong, and Wonju as major centers  
- a **ritual and artistic medium**, used in calligraphy, painting, sutras, and crafts  
- an **architectural material**, used in hanok windows and flooring  
- a **historical technology**, used for armor, documents, and royal archives  

Hanji is globally recognized for its preservation qualities and is used in museum conservation.

## Purpose of This Contract

The goal of this registry is to:

1. **Create a permanent, censorship-resistant record** of hanji papermaking traditions.  
2. **Preserve endangered artisanal knowledge** with technical accuracy.  
3. **Document materials, techniques, functional properties, and cultural context**.  
4. **Provide an immutable reference** for museums, researchers, artisans, and future generations.  
5. **Honor the regional diversity of Korean papermaking craftsmanship.**

The contract is intentionally:

- **ownerless** — no authority can alter or censor the registry  
- **payment-free** — no monetization, no barriers  
- **minimalistic and secure** — no external calls  
- **permanent** — entries become part of the cultural ledger  

## What Each Entry Records

- **paperType** — Jeonju hanji, Andong hanji, Wonju hanji, sutra paper, armor paper  
- **region** — Jeonju, Andong, Wonju  
- **materials** — mulberry bark, hollyhock mucilage, natural oils  
- **techniques** — webal, ssangbal, pounding, sheet formation  
- **functionalProperties** — durability, translucency, preservation quality  
- **culturalContext** — calligraphy, architecture, rituals, restoration  
- **uniqueness** — regional identity, rare methods, historical uses
